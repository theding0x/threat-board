STATE OF HAWAII 


























A BILL FOR AN ACT 





RELATING
TO FIREARMS . 





BE IT ENACTED BY THE LEGISLATURE OF THE STATE OF HAWAII: 










SECTION 1. The legislature finds that gun violence is a
growing concern in Hawaii. Due to
multiple high profile shootings across the State, it is critical that the State
hold individuals accountable for illegally possessing firearms in public. "Ghost guns", which are untraceable
firearms that can be easily assembled with little to no training, are also
becoming more prevalent in gun violence. 
Individuals who illegally carry firearms or firearm parts in public must
be held accountable to reduce gun violence. 

Currently,
state and county law enforcement are unable to bring charges against an
individual in possession of a firearm that has been deconstructed, which makes
it difficult to reduce the potential for gun violence. This loophole is known to criminals and has
been used to evade enforcement of the gun laws as no charges can be sought
against a person in possession of all the parts necessary to create a firearm
because the weapon is not considered "operational". Even an unskilled person can assemble a deconstructed
firearm in a matter of seconds. There is
no reason for a person to be in possession of unsecured firearm parts as they
traverse through our state other than to assemble the parts to create a gun. This loophole of carrying gun parts must be
closed to protect the community from gun violence. 

Often,
individuals who illegally carry firearms throughout the State are not merely
trying to protect themselves; they are also committing a significant number of
other crimes, which create a significant risk to public safety. In 2023, the Honolulu police department
initiated one hundred forty-three cases for "place to keep" offenses,
of which eighty-four arrests were effected on seventy-two individuals. In 2023 alone, those seventy-two individuals
arrested for "place to keep" accounted for an additional two hundred
nineteen felony arrests and one hundred ninety-five misdemeanor arrests. 

In
recent years, shootings among teens and young adults have become increasingly
common in Honolulu. These teens and
young adults are not of age to legally possess a firearm but have been able to
acquire the parts to build complete weapons because of the availability of
